Biomedical Engineering

Applying engineering principles to medicine and healthcare innovation

Biomedical engineering (BME) or medical engineering is the application of engineering principles and design concepts to medicine and biology for healthcare applications such as diagnostic or therapeutic purposes. BME integrates logical sciences to advance health care treatment, including diagnosis, monitoring, and therapy. This field combines knowledge from engineering, biology, chemistry, and medicine to create innovative solutions for healthcare challenges.

Overview and Scope

Biomedical engineering has recently emerged as its own field, as compared to many other engineering fields. Such an evolution is common as a new field transitions from being an interdisciplinary specialization among already-established fields to being considered a field in itself. Biomedical engineers manage current medical equipment in hospitals while adhering to relevant industry standards. This involves procurement, routine testing, preventive maintenance, and making equipment recommendations, a role also known as a Biomedical Equipment Technician (BMET) or clinical engineer.

Much of the work in biomedical engineering consists of research and development, spanning a broad array of subfields. Prominent biomedical engineering applications include the development of biocompatible prostheses, various diagnostic and therapeutic medical devices ranging from clinical equipment to micro-implants, imaging technologies such as MRI and EKG/ECG, regenerative tissue growth, and the development of pharmaceutical drugs including biopharmaceuticals.

Major Subfields and Applications

Bioinformatics

Bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field that develops methods and software tools for understanding biological data. It combines computer science, statistics, mathematics, and engineering to analyze and interpret biological data. Common uses of bioinformatics include the identification of candidate genes and nucleotides (SNPs), understanding the genetic basis of disease, analyzing unique adaptations, and identifying desirable properties in agricultural species. Bioinformatics also seeks to understand organizational principles within nucleic acid and protein sequences.

Biomechanics

Biomechanics is the study of the structure and function of the mechanical aspects of biological systems, at any level from whole organisms to organs, cells and cell organelles, using the methods of mechanics. Data obtained from crash test dummy impacts are integral to understanding biomechanical principles and improving safety in design.

Biomaterials

A biomaterial is any matter, surface, or construct that interacts with living systems. As a science, biomaterials is about fifty years old and has experienced steady and strong growth. Biomaterials science encompasses elements of medicine, biology, chemistry, tissue engineering and materials science. Many companies invest large amounts of money into the development of new biomaterial products.

Biomedical Optics

Biomedical optics combines the principles of physics, engineering, and biology to study the interaction of biological tissue and light, and how this can be exploited for sensing, imaging, and treatment. It has applications including optical imaging, microscopy, ophthalmoscopy, spectroscopy, and therapy. Examples include optical coherence tomography (OCT), fluorescence microscopy, confocal microscopy, and photodynamic therapy (PDT). OCT uses light to create high-resolution, three-dimensional images of internal structures such as the retina or coronary arteries. Adaptive optics is helping improve imaging by correcting aberrations in biological tissue.

Tissue Engineering

Tissue engineering is a major segment of biotechnology that overlaps significantly with BME. One goal is to create artificial organs via biological material such as kidneys and livers for patients needing organ transplants. Researchers have grown solid jawbones and tracheas from human stem cells. Several artificial urinary bladders have been grown in laboratories and transplanted successfully into human patients. Bioartificial organs, which use both synthetic and biological components, are also a focus area, such as hepatic assist devices that use liver cells within an artificial bioreactor construct.

Genetic Engineering

Genetic engineering, recombinant DNA technology, and gene splicing apply to the direct manipulation of an organism's genes. Unlike traditional breeding, genetic engineering utilizes modern tools such as molecular cloning and transformation to directly alter gene structure. Successful applications include improvement of crop technology, manufacture of synthetic human insulin through modified bacteria, production of erythropoietin in hamster ovary cells, and development of experimental mice such as the oncomouse for research.

Neural Engineering

Neural engineering (neuroengineering) uses engineering techniques to understand, repair, replace, or enhance neural systems. Neural engineers solve design problems at the interface of living neural tissue and non-living constructs. It can assist with prosthetics development, including cognitive neural prosthetics (CNP) which allow chip implants to assist people with prosthetics by providing signals to operate assistive devices.

Pharmaceutical Engineering

Pharmaceutical engineering is an interdisciplinary science that includes drug engineering, novel drug delivery and targeting, pharmaceutical technology, unit operations of chemical engineering, and pharmaceutical analysis. It may be deemed as part of pharmacy due to its focus on technology applied to chemical agents in providing better medicinal treatment.

Medical Devices and Equipment

An extremely broad category of biomedical engineering covers all health care products that do not achieve their intended results through predominantly chemical or biological means, and do not involve metabolism. Medical devices are intended for use in the diagnosis of disease or other conditions, and in the cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease.

Examples include pacemakers, infusion pumps, heart-lung machines, dialysis machines, artificial organs, implants, artificial limbs, corrective lenses, cochlear implants, ocular prosthetics, facial prosthetics, somato prosthetics, and dental implants. Stereolithography is a practical example of medical modeling being used to create physical objects.

Medical Device Classification

Medical devices are regulated and classified in the US as follows: Class I devices present minimal potential for harm and are often simpler in design, including tongue depressors, bedpans, elastic bandages, examination gloves, and hand-held surgical instruments. Class II devices are subject to special controls including special labeling requirements, mandatory performance standards, and postmarket surveillance, typically including non-invasive devices like X-ray machines, powered wheelchairs, and infusion pumps. Class III devices generally require premarket approval or notification and scientific review, including replacement heart valves, hip and knee joint implants, silicone gel-filled breast implants, and implantable pacemakers.

Medical Imaging

Medical/biomedical imaging is a major segment of medical devices, enabling clinicians to directly or indirectly view things not visible in plain sight. Imaging technologies often essential to medical diagnosis include fluoroscopy, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), nuclear medicine, positron emission tomography (PET), PET-CT scans, projection radiography such as X-rays and CT scans, tomography, ultrasound, optical microscopy, and electron microscopy. Navigation-guided equipment utilizes electromagnetic tracking technology, such as catheter placement into the brain or feeding tube placement systems.

Medical Implants

An implant is a medical device made to replace and act as a missing biological structure. The surface of implants contacting the body might be made of biomaterial such as titanium, silicone or apatite. In some cases, implants contain electronics like artificial pacemakers and cochlear implants. Some implants are bioactive, such as subcutaneous drug delivery devices or drug-eluting stents.

Bionics and Prosthetics

Artificial body part replacements are applications of bionics, which involves thorough study of human body systems' properties and functions. Careful study of eyes, ears, and other organs paved the way for improved cameras, television, radio transmitters and receivers. Artificial limbs demonstrate the intersection of mechanical engineering and biocompatible materials.

Biomedical Sensors

In recent years biomedical sensors based in microwave technology have gained attention. Different sensors can be manufactured for specific uses in both diagnosing and monitoring disease conditions. For example, microwave sensors can complement X-ray technology to monitor lower extremity trauma by detecting dielectric property changes in tissue during healing.

Clinical and Rehabilitation Engineering

Clinical Engineering

Clinical engineering is the branch of biomedical engineering dealing with implementation of medical equipment and technologies in hospitals or clinical settings. Major roles include training and supervising biomedical equipment technicians (BMETs), selecting technological products and services, logistically managing implementation, working with governmental regulators, and serving as technological consultants. Clinical engineers form a "bridge" between designers and end-users by combining perspectives of being close to point-of-use while trained in product and process engineering. One engineer per eighty beds in the hospital is the standard ratio.

Rehabilitation Engineering

Rehabilitation engineering is the systematic application of engineering sciences to design, develop, adapt, test, evaluate, apply, and distribute technological solutions for individuals with disabilities. Functional areas include mobility, communications, hearing, vision, cognition, and activities associated with employment, independent living, education, and community integration. Most rehabilitation engineers have undergraduate or graduate degrees in biomedical engineering, mechanical engineering, or electrical engineering. The rehabilitation process often entails design of assistive devices such as walking aids intended to promote inclusion into mainstream society and commerce.

Regulatory Framework and Standards

Regulatory issues have constantly increased in recent decades to respond to incidents caused by devices to patients. From 2008 to 2011 in the US, there were 119 FDA recalls of Class I medical devices. The medical device engineering area is among the most heavily regulated fields of engineering.

FDA Regulations

The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) is the principal healthcare regulatory authority in the United States, having jurisdiction over medical devices, drugs, biologics, and combination products. The greatest hurdles tend to be 510K "clearance" (typically for Class 2 devices) or premarket "approval" (typically for drugs and class 3 devices). Main regulatory objectives include ensuring products are safe, effective, and applicable to all manufactured devices. A product is safe if patients and users do not run unacceptable risks in intended use. A product is effective if it performs as specified by the manufacturer. These features must be ensured for all manufactured items through quality systems.

European Standards

In the European context, safety effectiveness and quality is ensured through "Conformity Assessment." The Medical Device Directive specifies procedures for Certification including tests and verifications in deliveries such as the risk management file, technical file, and quality management system. In the European Union, certifying entities named "Notified Bodies" are accredited by Member States. Once a product passes all steps required by the Medical Device Regulation, it is entitled to bear a CE marking, indicating it is believed to be safe and effective for marketing within the EU.

International Standards

The new International Standard IEC 60601 defines requirements for home healthcare electro-medical devices. IEC 60601-1-11 (2010) must be incorporated into design and verification of home use and point of care medical devices. AS/NZS 3551:2012 is the Australian and New Zealand standard for medical device management, covering procurement, acceptance testing, maintenance, and decommissioning. RoHS 2 (Directive 2011/65/EU) restricts hazardous substances in electrical and electronics devices, with scope widened to include medical devices and industrial equipment.

Education and Career Development

Biomedical engineers require considerable knowledge of both engineering and biology, and typically have a Bachelor's (B.Sc., B.S., B.Eng. or B.S.E.) or Master's (M.S., M.Sc., M.S.E., or M.Eng.) or doctoral (Ph.D., or MD-PhD) degree in BME or another branch of engineering with considerable BME overlap. Many engineering colleges now have a Biomedical Engineering Department or Program with offerings from undergraduate to doctoral levels.

Undergraduate Programs

In the U.S., an increasing number of undergraduate programs are recognized by ABET as accredited bioengineering/biomedical engineering programs. As of 2023, 155 programs are currently accredited by ABET. Many students use a B.Sc. in Biomedical Engineering which includes biological science content as a "pre-med" major in preparation for medical school. In Canada and Australia, accredited graduate and undergraduate programs are common, with universities like McMaster University offering M.A.Sc, MD/PhD, and PhD degrees, and the University of Guelph offering the first Canadian undergraduate BME program as a four-year B.Eng.

Graduate Education

Graduate education is a particularly important aspect in BME. While many engineering fields do not need graduate-level training for entry-level positions, the majority of BME positions prefer or require graduate degrees. Since most BME professions involve scientific research in pharmaceutical and medical device development, graduate education is almost a requirement. Some undergraduate BME programs actively discourage students from majoring without expressing intention to obtain a master's degree or apply to medical school. Graduate programs in BME are highly varied and may emphasize certain aspects, featuring extensive collaboration with medical schools or other engineering divisions due to BME's interdisciplinary nature.

Licensure and Certification

Each state has certain requirements for becoming licensed as a registered Professional Engineer (PE). In the US, the Fundamentals of Engineering exam now covers biology. For the Professional Engineering exam, candidates may select a particular engineering discipline, though there is currently not a BME option, meaning biomedical engineers must prepare in another category. The Biomedical Engineering Society (BMES) is exploring implementing a BME-specific version. Beyond governmental registration, private-sector organizations offer certifications such as the Certified Clinical Engineer (CCE) certification for clinical engineers. In the UK, mechanical engineers in Medical Engineering areas can gain Chartered Engineer status through the Institution of Mechanical Engineers.

Employment and Future Outlook

In 2012 there were about 19,400 biomedical engineers employed in the US, and the field was predicted to grow by 5% (faster than average) from 2012 to 2022. Biomedical engineering has the highest percentage of female engineers compared to other common engineering professions. As of 2023, there are 19,700 jobs for this degree, with average pay around $100,730 and approximately $48.43 per hour. There is expected to be a 7% increase in jobs from 2023 to 2033 (even faster than the previous average). The number of biomedical engineers is expected to rise as both a cause and effect of improvements in medical technology.
