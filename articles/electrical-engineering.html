Electrical Engineering

The study, design, and application of electricity, electronics, and electromagnetism

Electrical engineering is an engineering discipline concerned with the study, design, and application of equipment, devices, and systems that use electricity, electronics, and electromagnetism. It emerged as an identifiable occupation in the latter half of the 19th century after the commercialization of the electric telegraph, the telephone, and electrical power generation, distribution, and use.

Scope and Fields

Electrical engineering is divided into a wide range of different fields, including computer engineering, systems engineering, power engineering, telecommunications, radio-frequency engineering, signal processing, instrumentation, control engineering, photovoltaic cells, electronics, and optics and photonics. Many of these disciplines overlap with other engineering branches, spanning a huge number of specializations.

Electrical engineers typically hold a degree in electrical engineering, electronic or electrical and electronic engineering. They work in a very wide range of industries and the skills required are likewise variable, ranging from circuit theory to project management skills.

History

Electricity has been a subject of scientific interest since at least the early 17th century. William Gilbert was a prominent early electrical scientist and is credited with establishing the term "electricity." In the 19th century, research intensified with notable developments including the work of Hans Christian Ørsted, who discovered in 1820 that an electric current produces a magnetic field, and Michael Faraday, the discoverer of electromagnetic induction in 1831.

Electrical telegraphy may be considered the first example of electrical engineering. The profession emerged in the later 19th century as practitioners created a global electric telegraph network. The first professional electrical engineering institutions were founded in the UK and US. By the end of the 19th century, the world had been forever changed by rapid communication made possible through land-lines, submarine cables, and wireless telegraphy.

The 20th century saw revolutionary developments including the transistor invented at Bell Telephone Laboratories in 1947, the integrated circuit in 1959, and the MOSFET (metal-oxide-semiconductor field-effect transistor) in 1959, which became the basic building block of modern electronics and enabled the microprocessor revolution.

Education and Professional Practice

Electrical engineers typically possess an academic degree with a major in electrical engineering or electronics engineering. The length of study is usually four or five years, resulting in a Bachelor of Engineering or Bachelor of Science degree. The program includes units covering physics, mathematics, computer science, project management, and various topics in electrical engineering.

After completing a certified degree program, engineers must satisfy a range of requirements including work experience before professional certification. Once certified, engineers are designated titles such as Professional Engineer, Chartered Engineer, or European Engineer depending on the country. Professional bodies include the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) and the Institution of Engineering and Technology (IET).

Key Subdisciplines

Power and Energy Engineering

Deals with the generation, transmission, and distribution of electricity as well as the design of transformers, electric generators, electric motors, and power electronics. Engineers work on power grids and both on-grid and off-grid power systems.

Telecommunications Engineering

Focuses on the transmission of information across communication channels such as coax cable, optical fiber or free space. Engineers design transmitters and receivers for communication systems and must balance cost and performance considerations.

Control Engineering

Focuses on modeling dynamic systems and designing controllers using feedback mechanisms. Applications range from flight and propulsion systems of commercial airliners to cruise control in automobiles and industrial automation.

Electronics Engineering

Involves the design and testing of electronic circuits using components such as resistors, capacitors, inductors, diodes, and transistors. Modern integrated circuits pack millions of tiny electrical components into chips the size of a coin.

Signal Processing

Deals with the analysis and manipulation of analog and digital signals. Applications include audio equipment, telecommunications, radar systems, and digital media compression. DSP processors are found in devices from smartphones to missile guidance systems.

Computer Engineering

Deals with the design of computers and computer systems, including both hardware and software aspects. Computer-like architectures are now found in embedded devices including video game consoles, DVD players, and robots.

Modern Applications and Tools

From the Global Positioning System to electric power generation, electrical engineers have contributed to the development of a wide range of technologies. They design telecommunications systems, operate electric power stations, design lighting and wiring of buildings, create household appliances, and develop electrical control systems for industrial machinery.

Fundamental to the discipline are physics and mathematics. Today most engineering work involves computers, with computer-aided design programs being commonplace. Engineers use various instruments including multimeters, oscilloscopes, spectrum analyzers, and network analyzers depending on their specialization.

Recent developments include electrical engineering's integration with machine learning and artificial intelligence, particularly in fields such as signal processing, image processing, control engineering, electronic design automation, and smart grids.

Career and Workforce

In Australia, Canada, and the United States, electrical engineers make up around 0.25% of the labor force. The workplaces of engineers are varied, from pristine lab environments of fabrication plants to naval ships, consulting firm offices, or mining sites. Engineers may supervise scientists, electricians, computer programmers, and other engineers throughout their careers.

Technical work often accounts for only a fraction of an engineer's responsibilities, with significant time spent on proposals, budgets, project schedules, and team management. Strong written communication and project management skills are therefore essential for career advancement.
